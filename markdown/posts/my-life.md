Recently, a lot of folks on the internet have shared how work looked like for their parents or grandparents. All
to rethink what AI means for our life. I'm not sure if I can do that, as most of my arguments will be derived from
[a previous post](https://www.elias.sh/posts/the_burnout_machine), so I will try to skip the AI bits and focus on my family.

My grandfather died while working. Really, he worked at a construction site. He had an heart-attack.
My father worked on the same place.
This happened years before I was born, but I have a vivid memory as I was there thanks to a history told once
 when I was a kid: My father learning what was happening and jumping off the office window to run in to the ambulance.

Work can kill you.

My uncle worked in an factory dealing with very toxic material. The owners, to increase profit, did not provided all 
security equipment, and they forced the employees to work overtime off the books. My uncle died of Cancer a years after
he started work on that place.

Work may be killing you, a bit at a time.

My father on the other hand is alive and very close to retire. If this was a right-wing blog, I would probably be focusing
on him on this text: he didn't even finish middle school, but by his 30's he was doing at work all the planning the engineers
couldn't do properly or fast enough. Even tho he was more than capable of handling all the theory
he learnt at work, the only way to keep progressing salary wise was to get a degree. So he took night classes, finished middle school, high school and started college. He started his work with no computers, and he adapted himself on each advance of his field.

If you endure enough, you can survive.

I promised I wasn't going to talk about AI, I lied. I think we all have to watch if AI is something that will kill us, 
slowly make our health worse, or something we must endure.
